## What a Savings Goal Calculator Does (and Doesn't Do)

A savings goal calculator answers two questions: how much do I need to save each month to reach my goal by a certain date, and how long will it take to reach my goal if I save a fixed amount each month?

It is not the same as a compound interest calculator. A compound interest calculator starts with a known contribution and shows you what it grows to. A savings goal calculator starts with a known target and works backward to find the required contribution.

## How to Calculate How Long to Save a Specific Amount

Here are the steps:

1. Decide on your savings goal amount (for example, $10,000 for an emergency fund or down payment)
2. Choose your monthly contribution (how much you can realistically set aside each month)
3. Enter the interest rate your savings will earn (4-5% for a HYSA in 2026, 0% if keeping cash)
4. The calculator outputs the number of months to reach your goal

**Worked example:** $10,000 goal, $400 per month, 4.5% annual interest rate (HYSA) = 22 months to reach the goal. Without interest at the same contribution, it would take 25 months.

## How Much Interest Rate Actually Matters for Short-Term Goals

For goals under 12 months, the interest rate has almost no effect on the outcome. For goals over 18 months, a higher rate meaningfully reduces how much you need to contribute each month.

| Goal: $10,000 in 2 years | Required monthly contribution |
|--------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 0% (cash under mattress) | $417/month |
| 3% (standard savings)    | $402/month |
| 4.5% (HYSA)              | $394/month |
| 5% (HYSA high end)       | $391/month |

The difference between 0% and 5% over 2 years is about $26 per month. Meaningful but not dramatic. Where interest rate becomes critical is for goals over 5 years — at that point, use a compound interest calculator rather than a savings goal calculator.

## Savings Goals for Variable Income Earners

If your income varies month to month, a fixed monthly savings target is harder to commit to. An alternative approach: save a percentage of every payment received rather than a fixed dollar amount.

For example, if you earn $3,000 one month and $6,000 the next, saving 15% of each payment ($450 and $900) gets you further than committing to $500 and failing in a slow month.

## Separate Accounts for Separate Goals: Why It Works

Research by economist Richard Thaler (who won the Nobel Prize in Economics in part for this work) showed that people are significantly better at reaching financial goals when they keep separate accounts for separate purposes. The psychological separation of "this money is for the car" vs "this is everything I have" makes it harder to rationalize spending it on something else.

Practical application: open one HYSA for your emergency fund, a separate HYSA for your down payment, and a separate account for your vacation fund. Run each through the savings goal calculator independently. The total monthly transfer is the same — the outcome is dramatically different.

## Common Savings Goal Pitfalls

**Not automating:** Manual transfers get skipped. Set up automatic transfers the day after your income lands, before you spend it.

**Not accounting for interest:** Ignoring the interest earned on a 2-year HYSA goal means you will save more than you need to. Enter a rate to get a more accurate target.

**One account for all goals:** When the emergency fund and vacation fund are in the same account, a car repair "borrows" from the vacation fund. Separate accounts prevent this.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What if I miss a month?**
Recalculate. Enter your current balance, your remaining goal amount, and the time left. The calculator will adjust your required monthly contribution. Do not try to "make up" a missed month by doubling down — just resume normally.

**How do I factor in inflation?**
For goals under 2 years, inflation has minimal impact on the nominal amount you need. For longer-term goals (a down payment in 5 years on a house that will cost more by then), add your expected inflation rate to your goal amount before entering it into the calculator.

**Savings goal calculator vs compound interest calculator: which do I use?**
Use the savings goal calculator when you know what you want and when you want it, and need to find the monthly contribution. Use the compound interest calculator when you know what you will contribute and want to see how much it grows to.
