Compound interest with monthly contributions is one of the most powerful forces in personal finance. Each monthly deposit starts its own compounding clock, which is why contributing consistently beats making a single lump sum and waiting.

Use our [compound interest calculator](/calculators/compound-interest) to see exactly how your contributions grow over time.

## What Is Compound Interest? (40-Word Version)

Compound interest is interest earned on both your principal and your previously earned interest. Unlike simple interest, which only applies to your original deposit, compound interest grows exponentially because each interest payment adds to the base for the next calculation.

### Simple Interest vs Compound Interest: The Actual Difference

With simple interest, $10,000 at 7% for 10 years earns $7,000 total. With compound interest, the same $10,000 at 7% for 10 years grows to $19,672 - nearly $3,000 more, without doing anything differently.

## Why Monthly Contributions Change Everything

A lump sum investment is powerful. Monthly contributions are more powerful. Here is why.

Every time you add money, that deposit begins its own compounding sequence. A $500 contribution today will compound for 30 years. A $500 contribution in month 2 will compound for 29 years and 11 months. Each deposit builds its own independent growth curve.

The result: $500 per month for 30 years at 7% grows to $567,000. A single $180,000 lump sum at 7% for 30 years grows to $1,370,000 - but most people cannot access $180,000 upfront. Monthly contributions democratize compound growth.

## The Rule of 72: Mental Math for Compound Growth

The rule of 72 gives you a quick estimate of how long it takes to double your money at a given rate.

**Formula:** 72 / interest rate = years to double

| Rate | Years to Double |
|------|----------------|
| 4% | 18 years |
| 6% | 12 years |
| 7% | 10.3 years |
| 8% | 9 years |
| 10% | 7.2 years |
| 12% | 6 years |

At the historical S&P 500 real return of 7%, your money doubles roughly every 10 years. This is why time in the market matters more than timing the market.

## What Interest Rate Should You Use?

The rate you enter determines everything. Here are reference points:

- **S&P 500 index fund (long-term average):** 10% nominal, 7% inflation-adjusted (real). Use 7% for retirement planning scenarios.
- **High-yield savings account (HYSA):** 4-5% in 2026. Use this for goals under 5 years.
- **Conservative bond portfolio:** 3-4%
- **Money market account:** 3-4%

For goals beyond 10 years, index fund returns (7% real) are historically reasonable. For anything under 5 years where you cannot afford to lose principal, use HYSA rates.

## UK Users: ISA and Compound Interest

For UK users, compound interest inside an ISA is tax-free. This means the rate you enter into the calculator is your actual take-home rate - there is no tax drag reducing your real return.

The 2026 ISA allowance is 20,000 GBP per tax year. Any interest or growth within an ISA is not subject to income tax or capital gains tax. Over long periods, this tax shelter significantly improves real returns compared to a standard investment account.

For reference, the [UK government ISA page](https://www.gov.uk/individual-savings-accounts) covers current allowances and eligible account types.

## Canadian Users: TFSA Compound Growth

The Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA) works similarly to the UK ISA. Growth, dividends, and withdrawals are all tax-free. The 2026 TFSA contribution limit is $7,000, or approximately $583 per month.

Any unused room from prior years accumulates, so many Canadians have significantly more contribution room than the current-year limit. Enter your TFSA interest or growth rate into the compound interest calculator to project your balance, knowing that the number you see is your actual take-home value.

## Common Mistakes That Kill Compound Growth

- **Withdrawing early:** Each withdrawal removes capital that was compounding. A $5,000 withdrawal at age 35 costs far more than $5,000 by age 65.
- **Using nominal instead of real returns:** 10% sounds better than 7%, but inflation erodes 3% annually. Use real returns for retirement projections.
- **Keeping long-term money in low-rate accounts:** Cash in a checking account at 0.01% is not compounding meaningfully. Move it to a HYSA or index fund appropriate to your timeline.
- **Stopping contributions during market dips:** Dips are when contributions buy more shares at lower prices - the worst time to stop.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does compounding frequency matter?**
Yes, but less than most people expect. Monthly compounding vs annual compounding at the same rate produces a small difference over long periods. What matters far more is the interest rate itself and how long the money compounds.

**What if I miss a month of contributions?**
Missing one month has a minimal long-term impact. Resume contributions the following month. The bigger risk is stopping entirely because of one missed payment.

**Can I use compound interest to pay off debt faster?**
Compound interest works against you on debt (interest compounds on what you owe). The compound interest calculator models growth, not debt payoff. Use our debt snowball calculator to model debt repayment.

**What is a realistic interest rate for a 30-year retirement projection?**
7% real (inflation-adjusted) based on historical S&P 500 data is a commonly used planning assumption. For a more conservative projection, use 5-6%.

**Does the compound interest calculator account for taxes?**
No - the calculator shows gross growth. For taxable accounts, subtract your estimated capital gains or dividend tax rate from your projected return. For ISA/TFSA/Roth IRA accounts, the projected balance is your actual take-home value.
