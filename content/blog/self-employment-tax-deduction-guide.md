Every legitimate deduction reduces your net self-employment income, which lowers both your self-employment tax (15.3%) and your federal income tax. That double reduction is what makes deductions so valuable for freelancers — a $1,000 deduction does not just save you $220 in income tax, it also saves you $153 in SE tax.

## How Self-Employment Deductions Work (The Mechanism)

When you work a salaried job, your employer withholds income tax but also pays half your FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare). As a freelancer, you pay the full 15.3% self-employment tax yourself on your net self-employment income.

Every dollar you deduct as a business expense reduces your net SE income. That reduction flows in two directions: lower SE tax (15.3% of the deducted amount) AND lower income tax (your marginal rate applied to the deducted amount). If you are in the 22% bracket, a $1,000 deduction saves you $153 + $220 = $373 total — more than a third of the deduction amount.

## The 50% Self-Employment Tax Deduction

Before anything else, there is an automatic deduction most new freelancers miss. The IRS lets you deduct half of your total SE tax as an above-the-line deduction on Schedule 1 of Form 1040.

If your SE tax is $9,180, you automatically deduct $4,590 — reducing your adjusted gross income (AGI) before even calculating your income tax bracket. This deduction exists because employees only pay half of FICA taxes; the employer pays the other half. The IRS gives freelancers a partial offset to account for paying both halves.

You do not need to itemize to claim this. It applies regardless of whether you take the standard deduction.

## Home Office Deduction: The Rules That Actually Apply

The home office deduction requires your workspace to meet the exclusive use test: the space must be used regularly and exclusively for business. A desk in your living room that you also use to watch Netflix does not qualify. A separate room used only for work does.

**Two calculation methods:**

*Simplified method:* $5 per square foot of your home office, up to 300 square feet. Maximum deduction: $1,500. No receipts beyond the square footage measurement required.

*Actual expenses method:* Calculate the percentage of your home dedicated to your office (office square footage / total home square footage), then apply that percentage to your total eligible home expenses (rent or mortgage interest, utilities, insurance, repairs). More paperwork, potentially larger deduction.

**Renting vs owning:** Both renters and homeowners can deduct the home office. If you rent, apply your office percentage to your monthly rent and utilities. (Source: [IRS Publication 587](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p587))

## Vehicle and Mileage Deductions

The 2026 IRS standard mileage rate is 67 cents per mile. You can use this rate for any miles driven for business purposes — driving to client meetings, supply runs, the post office for business mailings.

What does not count as business mileage: commuting from home to a regular office. Since most freelancers work from home, this distinction rarely matters.

**Standard mileage vs actual expenses:** The standard mileage method is simpler — multiply your business miles by 67 cents. The actual expenses method requires tracking all vehicle costs (fuel, insurance, registration, maintenance, depreciation) and applying your business-use percentage. Most freelancers find the standard rate simpler with comparable results.

Keep a mileage log. The IRS requires date, destination, business purpose, and miles for each trip. A simple spreadsheet or a free app like MileIQ handles this automatically.

## Health Insurance Deduction for Self-Employed Workers

Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of health insurance premiums for themselves, their spouse, and their dependents. This deduction is above-the-line (on Schedule 1), meaning it reduces your AGI regardless of whether you itemize.

**Eligibility rule:** You cannot claim this deduction for any month in which you were eligible for employer-sponsored health insurance through a spouse's employer. If your spouse has coverage available through their job, this deduction does not apply.

This deduction covers medical, dental, and vision premiums. It also covers premiums for children under age 27 even if they are not your dependents on your tax return. (Source: [IRS Publication 535](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p535))

## Retirement Contributions: SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k), and SIMPLE IRA

Retirement contributions are one of the most powerful deductions available to freelancers. Every dollar you contribute to a SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) reduces your taxable income dollar for dollar.

| Plan | 2026 Limit | Deadline | Admin Complexity |
|------|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| SEP-IRA | Up to 25% of net SE income, max $69,000 | Tax filing deadline (+ extensions) | Very low |
| Solo 401(k) | Employee: $23,500 + Employer: 25% of net SE income, combined max $69,000 | December 31 for new plan | Medium |
| SIMPLE IRA | $16,500 employee + 3% employer match | October 1 to open for current year | Low |

For a freelancer earning $80,000 net SE income, a SEP-IRA allows contributions of up to $20,000. That $20,000 deduction at a 22% bracket plus 15.3% SE tax saves approximately $7,460 in total tax.

## Business Expense Deductions: What Qualifies and What Doesn't

| Deductible | Not Deductible |
|-----------|----------------|
| Software subscriptions (Notion, Adobe, Figma) | Personal clothing (even if worn to client meetings) |
| Professional development (courses, books, conferences) | Gym memberships (unless personal trainer is your business) |
| Equipment (computer, desk, camera, microphone) | Personal meals |
| Professional services (accountant, attorney) | Pet care |
| 50% of business meals | Home repairs unrelated to office |
| Phone (business-use percentage) | Commuting costs |
| Internet (business-use percentage) | Personal entertainment |

For meals, you can deduct 50% of the cost for meals directly related to business (client dinners, meals during business travel). You cannot deduct your own lunch at your desk.

## How Deductions Change Your Quarterly Tax Payment

Deductions directly reduce the income you report to the IRS, which lowers both your SE tax and income tax obligations — and therefore your quarterly estimated payments.

**Example:** $80,000 gross income, $15,000 in deductions (home office, software, professional development, phone/internet percentage, health insurance).

- Net SE income after deductions: $65,000
- SE tax (15.3%): $9,945 (vs $12,240 without deductions)
- SE tax deduction (50%): $4,973 (automatic)
- Adjusted gross income: $60,027
- Federal income tax (after standard deduction): significantly lower

Total tax savings from $15,000 in deductions: approximately $5,600, or $1,400 per quarter.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need receipts for every deduction?**
Yes, the IRS requires documentation for business deductions. Keep receipts, bank statements, and invoices. For mileage, keep a log. For home office, document the square footage. Digital records are acceptable — a folder of PDF receipts and bank export files is sufficient for most audits.

**What if I mix personal and business use (phone, internet, car)?**
You can deduct the business-use percentage. If you use your phone 60% for business, deduct 60% of your phone bill. Be conservative and consistent — the IRS will ask about your methodology if audited.

**Can I deduct my phone as a business expense?**
Yes, the business-use percentage of your phone bill is deductible. This includes both the service plan and, if purchased for business use, the device cost (which may need to be depreciated or taken under Section 179).

**Can I take the standard deduction AND business expenses?**
Yes. Business expenses (home office, mileage, SE health insurance, SEP-IRA) are above-the-line deductions that reduce your AGI. They are completely separate from the standard vs itemized deduction decision. You can claim the standard deduction and still deduct all your business expenses.
