## What Is a FIRE Number?

A FIRE number is the total investment portfolio value needed to retire early and never work again, calculated by multiplying your annual living expenses by 25. If you spend $40,000 per year, your FIRE number is $1,000,000. At that portfolio size, you can withdraw 4% per year indefinitely, based on historical market returns.

## The 4% Rule: Where the 25x Formula Comes From

The 4% rule originates from research by financial planner William Bengen, published in the Journal of Financial Planning in 1994, and later validated by the Trinity Study (1998). The research analyzed historical stock and bond returns and found that a retiree withdrawing 4% of their portfolio annually, adjusting for inflation, had a success rate exceeding 95% over 30-year retirement periods.

The 25x rule is simply the inverse: 1 divided by 0.04 = 25. Your FIRE number is your annual expenses times 25.

**Important caveat:** The original research modeled a 30-year retirement. If you retire at 35, you may need a 50-year or longer retirement horizon. Some researchers suggest a more conservative 3.5% withdrawal rate (28.6x multiplier) for very early retirees to account for this extended timeline and sequence of returns risk.

## Lean FIRE, Fat FIRE, and Barista FIRE

Not everyone optimizing for financial independence wants the same lifestyle.

| Type | Annual Spend | FIRE Number | Who It Suits |
|------|-------------|------------|--------------|
| Lean FIRE | Under $40,000 | Under $1,000,000 | Frugal minimalists, low cost-of-living areas |
| Regular FIRE | $50,000-$80,000 | $1,250,000-$2,000,000 | Median-income lifestyle in retirement |
| Fat FIRE | $100,000+ | $2,500,000+ | Maintaining current lifestyle without compromise |
| Barista FIRE | $30,000-$50,000 | $750,000-$1,250,000 | Partial retirement + part-time work for benefits/extras |

Barista FIRE is particularly relevant for freelancers, who often want to reduce their client load rather than stop working entirely. A smaller FIRE number plus occasional project work is a realistic middle path.

## Coast FIRE: The Alternative for Freelancers

Coast FIRE means you have invested enough that compound growth alone will reach your full FIRE number by traditional retirement age — without any additional contributions. You only need to earn enough to cover current living expenses.

The math: if you need $1,000,000 by age 65 and you are 35, you need your portfolio to grow from X to $1,000,000 over 30 years at 7% real return. Working backward: $1,000,000 / (1.07^30) = $131,367. If you have $131,000 invested at 35, you have reached coast FIRE.

| Current Age | Coast FIRE Amount (for $1M goal at 65) |
|-------------|----------------------------------------|
| 25 | $66,000 |
| 30 | $93,000 |
| 35 | $131,000 |
| 40 | $184,000 |
| 45 | $258,000 |

## How Freelancers Should Approach FIRE Differently

Freelancers already have a key FIRE advantage: flexible income. Most FIRE strategies assume a binary choice between full employment and full retirement. Freelancers can reduce their client load, raise their rates, or pivot to lower-effort project types — creating a natural glide path to partial retirement.

Key differences for freelancers:

**Sequence of returns risk:** Variable income combined with variable portfolio returns is the highest-risk FIRE scenario. A market crash in year one of retirement, when you also had a slow income year, can permanently damage a portfolio. Build a larger cash buffer (2 years of expenses) before declaring yourself FIRE'd.

**Healthcare before 65:** Without employer coverage, healthcare costs can run $600-$1,200 per month in the US before Medicare eligibility. Budget this explicitly in your annual spending assumption — it changes your FIRE number significantly.

**Social Security:** Self-employed individuals pay into Social Security and are eligible for benefits. Your SE tax payments count. Factor in a conservative Social Security estimate (50-70% of the projected amount shown on ssa.gov) to reduce the portfolio size you actually need.

## Common FIRE Calculation Mistakes

**Using nominal instead of real returns:** 10% sounds better than 7%, but inflation erodes 3% annually. Over 30 years, the difference between using nominal and real returns produces a dramatically wrong FIRE date estimate. Use real (inflation-adjusted) returns.

**Ignoring sequence of returns risk:** Historical average returns are averages. You might retire into a 10-year bear market. FIRE calculations based purely on averages underestimate this risk. Consider the conservative 3.5% withdrawal rate if you are retiring before 45.

**Not budgeting healthcare pre-65:** A $1,000/month healthcare budget adds $300,000 to your effective FIRE number (25 x $12,000/year). Missing this makes FIRE calculations deeply unrealistic.

**Forgetting lifestyle inflation:** If your annual spending grows with income, your FIRE number grows too. Base your FIRE number on your actual retirement spending target, not your current income.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What if the 4% rule fails?**
Historical failure rates are low (under 5% over 30 years), but not zero. Mitigations: use a more conservative withdrawal rate (3.5%), maintain a 1-2 year cash buffer so you are not selling investments during down years, and keep flexible income options open (freelancers are well-positioned for this).

**Is FIRE realistic on an average income?**
Yes for lean or barista FIRE, especially in lower cost-of-living areas. The math works on any income — the variable is the timeline, not the feasibility. A median-income earner saving aggressively can reach $500,000-$1,000,000 within 15-20 years.

**Does the FIRE number include Social Security?**
No — the FIRE number is designed to be self-sufficient without Social Security. Any Social Security income you eventually receive reduces how much you need to withdraw from your portfolio, acting as a safety margin.

**How does inflation affect my FIRE number?**
The 4% rule already accounts for inflation by assuming you increase your withdrawals annually to keep pace. The real return rate (7%) is the inflation-adjusted return, so your FIRE number calculation does not need additional inflation adjustment.
